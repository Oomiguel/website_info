Black Hole

1.General Information

  • A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing, including light or other electromagnetic waves, has enough energy to escape its event horizon. The boundary of no escape is called the event horizon and it has no locally detectable features according to general relativity. Black holes act like ideal black bodies, reflecting no light and emitting Hawking radiation with a temperature inversely proportional to their mass. They were first considered in the 18th century by John Michell and Pierre-Simon Laplace and the first modern solution of general relativity was found in 1916 by Karl Schwarzschild. In the 1960s, theoretical work showed they were a generic prediction of general relativity.

  • Black holes are massive compact objects that form when massive stars collapse. They can grow by absorbing mass from their surroundings or by merging with other black holes. The presence of a black hole can be inferred through its interaction with other matter and electromagnetic radiation such as visible light. Astronomers have identified numerous stellar black hole candidates in binary systems and established that Sagittarius A*, at the core of the Milky Way galaxy, contains a supermassive black hole of about 4.3 million solar masses. 

    2.History

  • John Michell proposed the idea of a supermassive but invisible 'dark star' in 1784, which would form when a star's diameter exceeds the Sun's and its surface escape velocity exceeds the usual speed of light. However, modern physics discredits Michell's notion of a light ray shooting directly from a supermassive star being slowed down by gravity and then free-falling back to the star's surface.

  • Albert Einstein's theory of general relativity was developed in 1915. Karl Schwarzschild found a solution to the Einstein field equations that describes the gravitational field of a point mass and a spherical mass. Johannes Droste independently gave the same solution for the point mass. At the Schwarzschild radius, the solution became singular, meaning some of the terms became infinite. Arthur Eddington showed that the singularity disappeared after a change of coordinates, but Georges Lemaître realized that this meant the singularity was a non-physical coordinate singularity.

  • Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar calculated that a non-rotating body of electron-degenerate matter above a certain limiting mass has no stable solutions. Robert Oppenheimer and others predicted that neutron stars above another limit (the Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff limit) would collapse further for the reasons presented by Chandrasekhar. Observations of the neutron star merger GW170817 have refined the TOV limit estimate to 2.17 M. Oppenheimer and his co-authors interpreted the singularity at the boundary of the Schwarzschild radius as indicating that this was the boundary of a bubble in which time stopped. 

    3.Properties and structure

  • A black hole is a small celestial body that has only three independent physical properties: mass, electric charge, and angular momentum. When an object falls into a black hole, any information about the shape of the object or distribution of charge on it is evenly distributed along the horizon of the black hole, and is lost to outside observers. This behavior is similar to that of a conductive stretchy membrane with friction and electrical resistance, unlike other field theories such as electromagnetism. The information that is lost includes every quantity that cannot be measured far away from the black hole horizon, including approximately conserved quantum numbers. This behavior is known as the black hole information loss paradox.

  • Black holes are static solutions of mass without electric charge or angular momentum. They are often referred to as Schwarzschild black holes, and are the only vacuum solution that is spherically symmetric. The Kerr-Newman metric describes a black hole with both charge and angular momentum, and the total electric charge and angular momentum are expected to satisfy the inequality. 

     4.Formation and evolution

  • Black holes were initially dismissed by the general relativity community, but a minority of relativists persuaded researchers in the field that black holes were physical objects. Simulations of two black holes colliding Penrose demonstrated that an event horizon requires a singularity to form, and Hawking showed that many cosmological solutions have singularities without exotic matter.

  • The Kerr solution, no-hair theorem, and laws of black hole thermodynamics showed that the physical properties of black holes were simple and comprehensible, making them respectable subjects for research. Conventional black holes are formed by gravitational collapse of heavy objects, but they can also be formed by other processes.
