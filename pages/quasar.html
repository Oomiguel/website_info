Quasar

1.General Information

  • A quasar is an active galactic nucleus (AGN) that is sometimes known as a quasi-stellar object, abbreviated QSO. It is powered by a supermassive black hole with a mass ranging from millions to tens of billions of solar masses, surrounded by a gaseous accretion disc. The redshifts of quasars are of cosmological origin and the term originated from "quasi-stellar [star-like] radio source". The observed properties of a quasar depend on many factors, including the mass of the black hole, the rate of gas accretion, the orientation of the accretion disc relative to the observer, the presence or absence of a jet, and the degree of obscuration by gas and dust within the host galaxy.

  • Quasars are the most distant known structures in the universe, with the nearest known being 600 million light-years away. 

    2.History of observation and interpretation

  • Between 1917 and 1922, it became clear that some objects seen by astronomers were distant galaxies like the Milky Way. When radio astronomy began in the 1950s, astronomers detected a small number of anomalous objects with properties that defied explanation. These objects emitted large amounts of radiation of many frequencies, but no source could be located optically.

  • The spectral lines of these objects were also extremely strange and defied explanation. Some of them changed their luminosity very rapidly in the optical range and even more rapidly in the X-ray range, suggesting an upper limit on their size, perhaps no larger than the Solar System. Considerable discussion took place over what these objects might be, and they were described as "quasi-stellar radio sources", or "quasi-stellar objects" (QSOs) a name which reflected their unknown nature, and this became shortened to "quasar". 

    3.Properties

  • Quasars are spectra that have redshifts between 0.056 and 7.64 (as of 2021). They are between 600 million and 29.36 billion light-years away in terms of comoving distance. The power of quasars originates from supermassive black holes that are believed to exist at the core of most galaxies. Quasars are visible from extreme distances, being the most luminous objects in the known universe. The brightest quasar in the sky is 3C 273 in the constellation of Virgo.

  • It has an average apparent magnitude of 12.8 and an absolute magnitude of 26.7. From a distance of about 33 light-years, this object would shine in the sky about as brightly as the Sun. Quasars are quasars that have a luminosity of about 4 trillion (41012) times that of the Sun, or about 100 times that of the total light of giant galaxies like the Milky Way. They are assumed to be radiating energy in all directions, but the active galactic nucleus is believed to be radiating preferentially in the direction of its jet. Quasars were much more common in the early universe than they are today, and Maarten Schmidt's discovery in 1967 was early strong evidence against steady-state cosmology and in favor of the Big Bang cosmology.

  • Quasars show the locations where supermassive black holes are growing rapidly (by accretion). Detailed simulations reported in 2021 showed that galaxy structures, such as spiral arms, use gravitational forces to 'put the brakes on' gas that would otherwise orbit galaxy centers forever, allowing the gas to fall into the supermassive black holes, releasing enormous radiant energies. These black holes co-evolve with the mass of stars in their host galaxy in a way not fully understood at present. The jets that produce strong radio emission in some quasars at the centers of clusters of galaxies are known to have enough power to prevent the hot gas in those clusters from cooling and falling on to the central galaxy.
